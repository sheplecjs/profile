People are gently puzzled when they learn I studied anthropology and now work with data for a living, as if the two belonged to different people who happen to share a name. I've stopped explaining it as a pivot. The most useful thing I know about data I learned in a trench in southwestern Saudi Arabia, and it arrived, after six weeks, in the form of a Pepsi can.

The dig was at Jurash. We went in expecting the usual rhythm of a season — slow, patient, the occasional sherd to reward the patience. What we got was nothing. Week after week of careful, stratified excavation and almost no finds: a few fragments of brick that couldn't have been more than a few hundred years old, and otherwise dirt. We kept going, because that is what you do, and somewhere in the going a quiet story took hold among us. The site was poor. Marginal. People had passed through but never stayed. Nobody said it out loud, exactly, but it had become the frame through which every empty bucket of soil made sense.

Then, in the main trench, right down on bedrock — the floor below which there is nothing older to find — a colleague's trowel turned up the season's one perfectly preserved artifact. A Pepsi can. Modern, intact, sitting almost on the bedrock it had no business being near.

For a moment it was just absurd, the punchline to six weeks of labour. But the can was not a disappointment. It was the only real finding we made — because it was not data about the site at all. It was data about the dig. A modern can does not sink to bedrock on its own. Someone had been there before us, gone all the way down, and backfilled. The site had been excavated decades earlier and the records misplaced, and we had spent six weeks meticulously interpreting ground that someone else had already emptied. The absence we'd been reading so carefully was never a fact about the ancient world. It was the fingerprint of a previous excavation we didn't know had happened. We had been interpreting a negative space as if it were a shadow cast by the past, when it was only the hole left by people who came before and took their notes with them.

That is the thing fieldwork actually trains, underneath the trowels. Not digging — provenance. Anthropology has spent a century being suspicious of exactly this: the gap between what a thing appears to say and what actually produced it. Its oldest caution is that what people tell you and what people do are two different datasets, and the truth lives in the distance between them. Its second oldest is that an absence is evidence — a thing that is missing is not a blank for you to fill with the story you brought to the field; it is a fact, and it demands an explanation as much as any presence does. Neither idea is particularly novel, they are just unusually portable.

They port, in particular, straight into data. Every dataset has a history before you arrive at it, and most of that history is invisible by the time it reaches your hands. Someone upstream filtered, joined, sampled, de-duplicated, dropped the rows that looked wrong to them for reasons they never wrote down. The clean, quiet, unremarkable table you've been handed is very often not a picture of the world but the fingerprint of that processing — the barren trench, dressed up as a finding. The flat line you are about to explain with a confident narrative about user behaviour is, more often than is comfortable, the shadow of a job that ran upstream and emptied the ground.

And the Pepsi can has a data form too. It is the single row that does not belong — the value that is impossible, the timestamp from before the company existed, the count that cannot be. The reflex is to treat it as contamination and quietly drop it so the model fits the story. But the row that breaks the frame is usually the most honest thing in the table. It isn't noise; it is the can on the bedrock, telling you that someone has been here before and that the absence you were about to interpret is an artifact of their visit. Dismissing it to protect your hypothesis is precisely the six-week mistake, run faster.

The real failure in that trench was never being wrong on day one. It was that the interpretation, once it set, quietly organised everything we saw afterward. We stopped testing the story and started defending it, and every empty bucket became confirmation. The correction isn't cleverness. It is a plain habit: ask what would have to be true for this absence to mean what I think it means — and treat the thing that won't fit as the gift it usually is.

I dig in partitions now, SQL is my trowel. But the first question hasn't changed in all the years since. Before you trust the ground, find out who dug it first.
