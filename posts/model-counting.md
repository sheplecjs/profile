The trope of the shiny dashboard that serves simply as a place for a business stakeholder to download a flat file they put into a spreadsheet to do their own thing has always been a Dilbert comic, aggravating and true. What's relatively new to this time honored travesty is the latest claim to "self-service", the presence of agentic natural language features on platform. "Talk to your data", they say. Many it would seem still prefer to have the conversation elsewhere, away from any carefully crafted metadata and context data teams have provided as foundations to keep the outputs marginally reasonable.

And you cannot stop them. That is worth being honest about. The conversation is going to happen somewhere you are not — a chat window, a spreadsheet with a model bolted to the side, some agent nobody on the data team has heard of — and it is going to happen to a flat file that left the platform hours ago. Everything you built to keep that file honest stays behind. The metric definitions, the catalog, the lineage, the column descriptions written at some cost — none of it rides along in the export. What rides along is the data itself: its shape, its grain, and whatever you did or didn't do about the same fact appearing twice.

So the question stops being how to keep people on the governed path and becomes a colder one. When this data is read by a model I don't control, in a place I can't see, stripped of every piece of context I attached to it — what does it say on its own? The only governance that survives the trip is the kind baked into the shape of the data. And that is the oldest discipline we have. Codd's normal forms are, underneath the formalism, one long argument about never storing the same fact twice. Kimball's first instruction to anyone building a fact table is to declare the grain — decide what one row *means*, precisely, before anything else. Get the grain right and the data tells the truth about itself to anyone who asks, context or no context. Get it wrong and it misleads every reader equally: the analyst, the dashboard, and the model the CFO is talking to at eleven at night.

What the grain decides first is what gets conflated. A row that quietly means two things — a "session" that is sometimes a visit and sometimes a single click, an "order" that is sometimes the basket and sometimes the line item — is, to any model, permission to treat them as one. Inside the platform a good analyst hits that ambiguity and stops; they feel the join is wrong before they can say why, and the semantic layer is there to catch them if they don't. Out in the spreadsheet there is no analyst and no semantic layer. There is a model that feels nothing, reasoning confidently from a grain you declared by accident, and a stakeholder ready to believe the fluent paragraph it hands back.

Then there is redundancy, which is not neutral — it is weight. We assume a duplicate row is harmless as long as nothing is being summed: it is simply there twice, no arithmetic to corrupt. But a model summarising or scoring has no reliable way to discount it. Researchers have a name for the broader failure now — metacognitive myopia — the absence of any mechanism for noticing that two pieces of input are the same fact and counting them once. Repetition reads as salience. The thing that appears three times becomes the thing the model decides matters. A fan-out you would have spotted and fixed inside your own pipeline travels, silent and intact, into the export — and the model you never met treats your plumbing as emphasis.

The last part is the one that should worry you, because it is the one you will never see. A duplicated row in a report you own is a bounded, recoverable error: the number is too high, you trace the fan-out, you fix it, you re-run, there is a ground truth and a road back to it. Hand the same data to a model running several steps of judgement in a tool you have no visibility into, and the error stops being a number. It becomes a premise — something accepted on the first step and built on for every step after — inside a conversation you were never part of. There is no row to point at, no re-run that is obviously right, and no one is going to forward you the transcript. The cost of bad grain has moved from a wrong number you can trace to a confident story you will never read.

The discipline this forces is unglamorous. When I have designed the grain for event data that fed models downstream, the tempting move was always to flatten everything into one wide row and let the consumer work it out. What actually worked was duller and slower: decide that one row would be exactly one complete unit of judgement — one self-describing thing a reader could assess with no outside context — normalised so that no fact about it could appear more than once, and refuse to ship the table until that was true. Not because I knew which model would read it. Because I had to assume I wouldn't.

"Talk to your data," the platform says, and people do — just not where we hoped, and not with the model we vetted. We are not going to win the argument about where the conversation happens; we lost it the moment the export button became easier than the dashboard. What we still own is what the data says when it gets there. Build the grain so the data tells the truth about itself to a stranger, because a stranger is who will be asking.

They will talk to your data. The only thing you still control is what it says back when you are not in the room.
